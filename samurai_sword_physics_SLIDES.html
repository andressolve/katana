The Fundamental Equation

Everything about cutting comes down to one simple principle:

Pressure = Force √∑ Area

Pressure is the force applied per unit of area. It's what actually determines whether something cuts or just bounces off.

Force: How hard you push or swing (measured in Newtons)

Area: The size of the contact surface (measured in square millimeters)

Pressure: Force concentrated into that area (measured in Pascals or N/mm¬≤)

The smaller the area, the greater the pressure!
This is why sharp edges cut.

A sharp blade has an incredibly tiny contact area‚Äîso even moderate force creates enormous pressure.

The Wedge: A Simple Machine

A blade's edge isn't just thin‚Äîit's also shaped like a wedge, which is one of the six classical simple machines.

What a wedge does: It converts force applied in one direction (downward) into force in a different direction (sideways), pushing material apart.

When you push a wedge down into wood, the wood doesn't just compress‚Äîit gets split apart sideways.

A blade is a mobile wedge!
It converts your downward/forward force into sideways splitting force

The wedge angle matters:

  • Steeper angle (like an axe): More force multiplication, splits things wide open, but needs more initial force
  • Shallow angle (like a katana): Less splitting force, but cuts with much less resistance‚Äîperfect for slicing

What the curve does:

The Steel Secret: Hard Edge, Flexible Spine

Here's the engineering problem: You want a hard edge (stays sharp) but also a flexible spine (doesn't shatter).

These properties are normally opposites in metallurgy!

Hard Steel

Pros: Holds sharp edge

Cons: Brittle, can shatter

Soft Steel

Pros: Flexible, absorbs impact

Cons: Won't hold edge

Japanese smiths solved this: They made ONE blade with BOTH properties!

The secret: Differential hardening

By coating the spine with clay before heat treatment and quenching, smiths created:

  • Hard edge (~60 HRC hardness): Cools quickly, forms very hard steel
  • Soft spine (~40 HRC hardness): Cools slowly, stays flexible

This is materials engineering genius!
